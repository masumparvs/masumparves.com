No. 01 · A Letter from the Console

An introduction, between a sysadmin and the reader.

— By Masum Parves
Sysadmin · Dhaka, BD
— Filed under Profile
Working life
Introduction
— Reading time Four minutes

I am Masum — a Linux system administrator working out of Dhaka, Bangladesh, on the kind of infrastructure most people never see. When I do my job well, you do not notice it. Servers stay up. Sites load. Backups quietly run at 3 a.m. and finish before anyone arrives in the morning.

I came to this work through curiosity and stayed through care. Through student years I led teams, I built things, I taught myself the shell. Today I split my time between shared-hosting environments where I help small businesses keep their WordPress sites safe, and longer engagements where I build out cloud infrastructure on AWS, GCP, and bare-metal VPS.

I would rather do one server properly than ten servers in a hurry.

I am an extrovert by temperament — happiest in a team, but equally comfortable on a midnight call alone with a compromised box. I prefer read-only investigation before any destructive change. I write things down. I leave configs better than I found them. I treat your production environment the way I would want mine treated.

What follows in these pages is a record of the work I do — the services I offer, the tools I use, the way I approach a project. If any of it sounds like what you need, I would be glad to hear from you.

No. 04 · The Method

How we shall work, in four movements.

I. Movement One — Discovery

Read, before writing.

Every engagement begins with a read-only investigation. I examine logs, current configurations, file integrity, running services. I do not touch anything that could change state until we have agreed on a plan. Surprises in production are the kind of surprise nobody enjoys.

II. Movement Two — Plan

A map, before the territory.

You receive a written scope: what I will do, in what order, with rollback steps for each phase. You always know what is happening on your server, and you always know how to undo it.

III. Movement Three — Build

Quiet, careful execution.

I implement the work — installing, configuring, hardening, migrating — and I document every meaningful change as I go. The goal is not just to fix the present problem but to leave the system better understood than I found it.

IV. Movement Four — Handoff

The last mile.

Verification, monitoring setup, and a written handoff so you (or your team) can take over with confidence. I stay on call for follow-up questions, and I prefer long-term relationships to one-off transactions.
